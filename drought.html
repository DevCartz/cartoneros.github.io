Drought Safety Guide

What is a Drought?

A drought disaster is a prolonged period of little or no rainfall that leads to a shortage of water. This affects agriculture, drinking water supplies, ecosystems, and the economy. Unlike sudden disasters, droughts develop slowly and can last for months or even years.

Before the Drought

  1. Water conservation policies (limit waste, promote efficient use).
  2. Rainwater harvesting systems and water storage infrastructure.
  3. Drought-resistant crops and farming techniques.
  4. Drought monitoring systems to detect early warning signs.
  5. Public awareness campaigns on water-saving practices.
  6. Emergency plans for water rationing and supply management.

During the Drought

  1. Implement water restrictions (e.g., limit irrigation, car washing, etc.).
  2. Distribute emergency water supplies to affected areas.
  3. Support farmers with food, water, and financial aid.
  4. Monitor food and water quality to avoid health risks.
  5. Prevent wildfires and manage dry vegetation.
  6. Public communication about water-saving and health tips.

After the Drought

  1. Assess damage to crops, livestock, water sources, and communities.
  2. Restore ecosystems (e.g., replant trees, refill lakes and rivers).
  3. Support affected families with food, jobs, and financial help.
  4. Improve long-term water management and drought planning.
  5. Upgrade infrastructure for water storage and distribution.
  6. Review and strengthen policies to reduce future drought impacts.
